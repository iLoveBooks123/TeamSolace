Everyone has a bit of it: OCD

By The Solace Team | Read Time: 1 min

‚Üê Back to Blog

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) is a chronic mental health condition marked by a cycle of unwanted, intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive mental or physical behaviors (compulsions) performed in response to these thoughts. These symptoms are often time-consuming and can significantly interfere with daily life, relationships, and work.

The obsessions are persistent, unwanted thoughts, urges, or images that cause significant distress or anxiety. Common themes include fears of contamination (germs, dirt), the need for symmetry or order, aggressive or taboo thoughts, and excessive doubt. The compulsions are repetitive behaviors or mental acts that an individual feels driven to perform in response to an obsession. The goal is typically to reduce anxiety or prevent a feared outcome, though the behaviors are often excessive and logically unrelated to the concern. Common compulsions include excessive hand washing, checking (locks, stoves), counting, or arranging items.

The casual use of the phrase "I'm a little bit OCD" when describing a preference for tidiness or organization downplays the severity of the actual disorder. For those with a clinical diagnosis, OCD is a potentially debilitating condition that can be all-consuming and cause immense suffering. Symptoms may involve fears of contamination so intense a person damages their skin from washing, or checking rituals that make them hours late for work. While over 80% of the general population experiences occasional intrusive thoughts, only about 1% to 3% meet the diagnostic criteria for clinical OCD at some point in their lives.
